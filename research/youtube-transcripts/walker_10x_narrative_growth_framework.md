# Turn What You Say (Your Message & Narrative) Into 10x Growth

## TL;DR
A company's message and narrative are its ultimate leverage points for growth. By shifting the core narrative at Passetto away from tactical CMOs and directly toward strategic economic buyers (CEOs and CROs), the company generated more pipeline and revenue in the first 27 days of October than in the entire preceding nine months of the year combined—all while keeping the exact same content distribution channels, mix, and publishing volume. B2B SaaS organic strategy relies entirely on mastering problem diagnosis; when you can articulate a buyer's systemic pain points better than they can, you capture immediate high-value demand. Companies must treat narrative as an iterative art, testing shifts directly in sales conversations and accelerating insights through high-volume content loops before overhauling websites or products.

## [Transcript]

The hidden 10x weapon for your company is actually your message and narrative. Just in the month of October so far—and the month isn't even done yet—at my company, Passetto, we've booked and closed more revenue and generated more pipeline in the first 27 days of October than we had the entire rest of the year. That's nine months of work, and we did more in 26 days of October than we had in the first nine months of the year in terms of revenue and pipeline. 

My content mix and distribution has not changed at all. I produce the same amount of podcasts every week, I put them on LinkedIn, and they go on other channels. The content and distribution mix and volume has remained identical. The only thing that's changed is **what I am saying**. When you are able to nail that, it becomes the thing that gives you massive growth from zero to ten. 

I believe the impact might be less dramatic and fast at scale, but you could have the exact same reinvention at a $100 million ARR company by truly nailing your narrative. That has been a huge learning for me—it is pure data and an unarguable conclusion around the fact that what you say is actually the most important element inside of your content.

### Sourcing Feedback from the Right Audience
I want to talk through the process of how you get to this point. The first time I did it at my previous company, Refine Labs, it took me around 18 months to dial in a message that perfectly resonated with the market so that they felt the problem, took action, and moved. This time at my new company, Passetto, it took about eight months. 

Here are a few of the non-obvious, key insights I've learned along the way:

> **Who you get your feedback from drives your company strategy.**

There are a lot of companies that get their strategic feedback from the wrong layer of customers. That flawed feedback then dictates how they build their product, what go-to-market motions they run, how they invest in marketing, how they message the problem, and the level of stakeholders they solve for. If it's driven by the wrong person, you end up building the wrong company. You won't know that overnight. 

I entered this game at Passetto thinking that the CMO was going to be our target customer. After 10 months, I have learned that as powerful and capable as a CMO can be, they do not own the full go-to-market engine. GTM is owned by either the CEO or the Chief Revenue Officer (CRO), depending on the organizational reporting structure. A CMO cannot single-handedly fix a broken pipeline system; they can help the company see the problem, but the CEO or CRO actually has to execute the fix. 

When I made that structural narrative shift, everything changed:
* Immediately, you see the problem differently.
* Immediately, you message the problem differently.
* Immediately, you quantify the problem differently.

It comes down to understanding the details of the problem at such a profound level that you can explain it better than your customer can, and completely differently from how the market currently explains it. If the market was already explaining it well and the status quo worked, people wouldn't have the problem in the first place. 

As a first step, I see people reach so far out—saying, *"I want to build an AI company"*—instead of identifying the core business problem they are trying to solve with AI. That discipline around problem diagnosis is hugely impactful. Make sure you are getting feedback from the right person. You might not know who that is when you start; you have to be willing to iterate, learn, and recognize that the message in your content is the ultimate driver. 

It's the exact same logic I used when talking about paid advertising in 2020 and 2021: as good as your targeting parameters can be, if you say something the person consuming the ad doesn't care about, the ad is worth absolutely nothing. The same applies to events. If you pay a $50,000 sponsorship fee for your CEO to give a speech at a conference, and the speech sucks so badly that everyone walks out learning nothing, what was that $50,000 worth? The message dictates the financial return.

### Accelerating Learning through Content Volume
As I gain more experience and wisdom around this, I continue to believe heavily in content volume. When you produce more volume in the right way, all you are doing is accelerating the process to get to the core insights faster. It's like going to the gym once a week versus seven days a week—who is going to get in shape faster? Increasing your content publishing cycles is a massive way to compress the time it takes to gather market feedback.

Sometimes I dislike giving prescriptive advice on this topic because narrative development is fundamentally an artistic skill. People who do it exceptionally well are painting a million-dollar painting. The enterprise value created is massive, but because it relies more on art than pure science, it's hard to encapsulate into a rigid checklist. 

### Multi-Threaded Success at the Executive Level
Looking at our data from May forward, our Net Revenue Retention (NRR) and customer success ratings paint a stark picture: the companies that kick off their projects directly with the CEO are off the charts. They expand fast, the executive team is happier, and they get significantly better financial results. 

Conversely, when a project comes in strictly through a tactical CMO, we often get pushed down to a VP or director level, and the initiative stalls because marketing alone doesn't possess the cross-functional authority to fix a systemic pipeline architecture problem. Without that authority, you lose the flywheel of expansion and compounding results. 

We looked across roughly 25 companies we've worked with, and the ones led by a CEO are clearly the most successful customers from every single angle. The first couple started simply: a CEO called and said, *"Hey, I've been listening to your podcast, I think your framework is interesting, and I want you to speak with me and my CFO about going through this process."* 

We weren't actively fishing for CEOs initially; we still assumed marketing would own it. But if you don't put the content out there with a distinct message, that CEO never hears it, they never come inbound, and you never get the learning. The narrative creates the loop. Once you have that executive alignment, you can qualitatively dissect customer success, calculate metrics improvements, and pitch high-level solutions. I find myself pitching the CEO on a million-dollar expansion to fix a $250 million pipeline dysfunction for the upcoming fiscal year. 

As an entrepreneurial CEO, you have to be in the mud doing this work. It cannot be outsourced. You have to work directly with customers to prove the expansion process, the product offering, and the pricing structure work. You have to validate that your messaging lands effectively both in a live sales conversation and at a broader macro-narrative level. You have to use the product exactly like your customer would. 

When my team works on the 50% of accounts led directly by a CEO, it is engaging, fast-paced, and high-growth because our strategic ideas actually go into practice. The other accounts, led down-funnel, are a slog. A highly valuable, non-business signal for entrepreneurs is simply tracking which customers your team actually enjoys working with.

### The Message-Testing Hack: Change the Narrative, Not the Product
The biggest tactical takeaway is this: **change your narrative, not your product marketing.** 

Do not rewrite what your website says day one unless it's actively detrimental. Keep the site static, change your narrative first, let it push through your content channels, and run live sales conversations. Let the customer communicate their problem, present your new framework, and prove that it wins deals. Once you validate the message in live buyer environments, only then should you commit to updating your product marketing, website copy, and collateral. It is a completely risk-free, zero-cost way to test messaging changes before spending capital on broad rebranding efforts.

We saw this play out perfectly. We had customers who started with us in July, went through a two-to-three-month cycle, saw the impact, and established deep trust with leadership. Right after securing those expansions, I attended a major executive conference in Austin with over 500 GTM leaders—CEOs, CROs, CMOS, and RevOps executives. 

Conversing with them, the universal undertone was clear: *"We have a massive pipeline problem, and we don't know how to fix it."* The hidden sentiment from CROs and CEOs was a distinct lack of confidence that their current marketing leaders or internal teams could solve it. 

When you look at a business holistically, it breaks cleanly into new logo acquisition and customer expansion. You absolutely need to know your Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) and NRR to plan marketing responsibly, but the ultimate point of holistic measurement is to optimize the pipeline creation process. It’s a complete reframing. Focus entirely on the acute problem people are actively facing. If a company has a structural product-market fit issue causing their GRR to plummet from 90% down to 70%, hiring an agency for a million dollars won't fix their pipeline problem—they need to fix their product. 

Diagnosing the exact root cause is where most leadership teams struggle. If the diagnosis reveals severe dysfunction within the pipeline creation process—leading to low ROI, missed targets, a starving sales team, and lowered forecasts, even while marketing blindly celebrates hitting an arbitrary "MQL plan"—that dysfunction can be measured quantitatively with a single business metric. 

Once quantified, you can easily calculate exactly how many millions of dollars that leak is costing the enterprise every single quarter. Armed with that financial reality, executive teams can make a confident, calculated decision: is it worth investing a million dollars to permanently solve a repeating $50 million per quarter problem, or should they roll the dice and let their team try to patch it for the third unsuccessful time this year?
