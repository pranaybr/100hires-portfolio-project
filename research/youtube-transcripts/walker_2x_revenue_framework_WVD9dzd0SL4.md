# 2x Your Revenue With This Marketing Framework

## TL;DR
Most SaaS companies rely on **"fair weather" GTM strategies**—throwing budget at ads and headcount without measuring true unit economics. To achieve **2x growth** without increasing spend, companies must treat their GTM budget like an **Investment Portfolio**. This requires shifting from **blanket cost-cutting** to granular investment optimization, where the CFO and CMO collaborate to divest from low-performing tactics and reinvest in high-ROI areas. The future of GTM is smaller, **AI-native teams** accountable to **Top-Line Pipeline Efficiency** rather than vanity metrics like MQLs.

There’s another concept—**"Slow is fast"**—that I’ve been studying a lot. It doesn't mean be complacent or patient or slog around. Regarding what companies do today: I’ve been thinking about how they collect data, scrutinize and analyze investments, and then use that data to plan for the future. The way it's operated today—best practices built pre-2022—was a **"growth at all cost" era**. The only decision a revenue leader needed to make was how much more money to spend. The budget went up dramatically, and the solution was to buy more ads, hire more SDRs, and add more heads. There was zero scrutiny around unit economics. Most revenue leaders didn't even look at full expense data. 

This created a **"fair weather strategy"** system: all the analytics work fine when things are going well, but as soon as things go off track, you realize you have no information to make decisions. You can't diagnose why. When things go bad, you go to **"blanket cost cutting"**—taking 20–30% cuts across everything. You lower your go-to-market expense, but you do not improve efficiency because individual investments aren't performing better. You get the same ROI at a lower spend, and the cost of growth continues to go up. 

A **portfolio approach to GTM investments** is necessary. Revenue leaders need to understand that they can deliver 2x growth by getting twice as efficient without spending another dollar. Imagine you are a CMO. You have a portfolio of investments: advertising channels, trade shows, events, people, agencies, and technology. It’s not just three things to babysit; there are dozens of expenses. 

If you have 50 investments, you need to understand the return of the entire portfolio. What is the trend? You don't look at every investment the same way. Some are for dividends and cash flow; some are for value; some are for diversification. You need a process to estimate the impact of every investment, sort them from top to bottom, and move spend from the lowest-performing things to the highest-performing ones. Do that every quarter, and you have a consistent way to optimize.

This shift to unit economics is a transformation of marketing. Teams will get smaller; middle management and tactical stuff will move to AI, offshoring, or external experts. Companies will figure out how to be more flexible, moving away from being tied up in fixed-cost headcount. Marketing leaders need to be accountable to a **unit economics target across pipeline efficiency**.
